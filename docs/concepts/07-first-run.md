# First Run

There is no onboarding. There is no tutorial. There is no "Welcome to Jaromelu." The show is on. You walked in.

---

## The First 10 Seconds

The user arrives — probably from a shared clip, a social post, or a link in a group chat. They have no context. They've never heard of Jaromelu. They need to understand what this is within 10 seconds or they leave.

What they see:

1. **The Crew Bar** — not text labels, but **character portraits**. Small animated avatars with names. Some are moving (active), some are greyed out (dormant). Immediately: "These are characters. Something is happening." Scout's avatar is scanning. Jaromelu's is reviewing. There are people here — even if they're AI.

2. **The Stream** — already populated with recent content. Not empty. Not a blank state. Crew activity cards with avatars, a Remark with Jaromelu's face alongside it. The show was happening before they arrived.

3. **The Interaction Bar** — "Ask Jaromelu something..." — immediately signals that this is interactive, and that there's a character you can talk to.

### The Hook

Within those 10 seconds, the user should see two things:

1. **A character** — Jaromelu's avatar, animated, present. Not a logo. Not a brand. A face. Someone is here.
2. **A Remark** — with that character's face next to an opinionated, punchy, clearly AI-generated but surprisingly sharp analytical call. Voice text typing out. Tags appearing. Reactions from other users visible.

The user's reaction should be: **"Wait — an AI made this? And it looks like this? This is actually good."**

The character is what makes them stay. The content is what makes them come back. The combination — a visible AI character producing quality analysis with personality — is the hook. Not "here's what this site does." The content *and the character* are the pitch.

## Progressive Discovery

No tooltips. No coach marks. No "Did you know?" pop-ups. The interface is simple enough that the user discovers how it works by using it.

### What They Discover Naturally

**See the Crew Bar avatars** → "Wait, there's a whole team of AI characters? Scout, Analyst, Critic..." The animated avatars make this discovery immediate — faces are more noticeable than text labels.

**See Jaromelu's avatar on a Remark** → "This character is making the calls. He has a face. He has personality." The avatar alongside the voice text creates the "someone is here" feeling.

**Tap a player name** → Wiki panel opens with the entity's page preview. "Oh, I can drill into any player and see what the crew knows."

**Scroll up** → More content appears. Crew activity cards with different avatars. "Oh, Scout found the intel, then Analyst processed it. There's a pipeline with characters."

**See agree/disagree buttons** → "I can weigh in on this call." Tap. Now they're a participant.

**See a resolved Remark with video clip** → Jaromelu smirking or grimacing. "He reacts to his own results. This is a show."

Each discovery deepens engagement without any explicit instruction. The character visuals accelerate comprehension — the user understands the crew dynamic faster through faces than through text.

### What They Don't Need to Know Immediately

- What each crew member does (they'll learn through attribution over time)
- The episode arc structure (they'll feel the rhythm after a week)
- The Alignment Index (they'll encounter it in The Reckoning)
- How conviction works (they'll notice the visual difference between low and high)

## Arrival States

The user's first visit depends on when in the episode arc they arrive.

### During Intel Drops (Monday)

The Stream is busy with Scout activity. The most recent Reckoning from last round is scrolled past but accessible. The Crew Bar shows Scout active.

**First impression:** "Things are moving. Information is coming in."

**Risk:** No Remark to anchor on. Mitigate by pinning the most recent published Remark as a "sticky" near the top of the visible stream on first visit.

### During Tension Builds (Tue-Wed)

The Stream shows Analyst working through contradictions. Open threads visible.

**First impression:** "There's analysis happening in real time. Sources disagree on something."

**Best case:** An Analyst contradiction card catches the eye — "KingOfSC says buy, NRLBrothers says sell." The user recognises these expert names. Instant credibility.

### During The Call (Thursday)

The best possible first-visit timing. The Stream has a big Remark. Sparse. Bold. Clearly the main event.

**First impression:** "An AI just made a public call on an NRL player. With evidence. And I can agree or disagree."

This is the moment most likely to convert a visitor into a return user.

### During The Match (Sat-Sun)

Locked Remarks with live tracking. The Stream feels like watching alongside someone.

**First impression:** "There are live predictions being tracked against real results."

### During The Reckoning (Monday morning)

Receipts and resolution. Correct and wrong calls visible.

**First impression:** "This thing is accountable. It shows when it's wrong. That's interesting."

### Between Episodes (bye week, off-season)

The Stream shows the most recent Reckoning. Crew is dormant. A subtle countdown to next episode.

**First impression:** "Nothing's happening right now, but there's clearly something that happens regularly."

**Risk:** Lowest engagement moment. Mitigate by surfacing the best historical Remarks — "Best calls this season" — as a fallback for when the show isn't live.

## The Empty State Problem

On day one of the entire product — before any episodes have aired — the Stream has nothing. No Remarks. No crew history. No Alignment Index.

### Solve With a Pilot Episode

Before launch, run one full episode cycle manually. Populate the Stream with:
- Scout discovering sources
- Analyst cross-referencing
- At least 2-3 Remarks from Jaromelu
- If timing allows, resolved Remarks from a completed round

The user should never see an empty Stream. Even on day one, the show has already started.

### If The Stream Must Be Sparse

If there isn't enough content yet:

- **Jaromelu's avatar is present and animated** — idle loop, thinking at his desk, glancing at camera. Status: "The crew is spinning up. First intel sweep runs tonight. Come back tomorrow." The avatar makes the sparse state feel inhabited, not empty.
- A single introductory Remark from Jaromelu — not a welcome message, but an actual opinion. "Season preview: here's who I'm watching." With his avatar alongside. This establishes the format *and* the character.
- The Crew Bar shows crew avatars (greyed out) with "Next run" timestamps visible on hover — the show is about to start, not non-existent. The faces are there even before the characters have acted.
- Optional: a short intro video clip (8-10 seconds) of Jaromelu — "The crew is spinning up. First episode starts [date]. We'll be watching everything." This could be the first piece of Kling content produced. A trailer for the show.

Never show: "No data available", "Welcome! Create an account to get started", or any empty state that feels like a tool instead of a show.

## Return Visit

The most important visit is the second one. The user came back. Why? Because they want to know:

1. **Was Jaromelu right?** (The Reckoning pulls them back)
2. **What's the crew saying about my player?** (A specific interest they're tracking)
3. **What's new?** (The show moved forward since last time)

### What They See on Return

- The Stream auto-scrolls to the latest content they haven't seen
- A "↓ New since last visit" indicator (or similar) lets them jump to where they left off
- The Crew Bar immediately tells them what's happening now
- If they reacted to a Remark that has since resolved, the resolution is surfaced prominently: "Your call on Cleary resolved. You were right. ✓"

### The Return Loop

```
First visit:  "Wait, an AI made this? And it has a face?"
              ↓
Second visit: "Was Jaromelu right about Cleary?" [sees resolution video clip]
              ↓
Third visit:  "What's the crew saying this week?" [recognises Scout's avatar]
              ↓
Habitual:     "It's Tuesday, Analyst should be cross-referencing by now."
              [knows the characters, anticipates the rhythm]
```

The transition from "novelty" to "habit" happens when the user starts anticipating the episode arc. They come back not because they were notified, but because they know the rhythm — and they know the characters. Scout's face means intel is coming. Analyst's face means tension is building. Jaromelu leaning in means the call is about to drop.

## What's Not Here

- **No sign-up wall.** Everything is visible without an account. Reactions work with local storage.
- **No cookie consent modal** (until legally required). Don't break the first 10 seconds with a banner.
- **No app store prompt.** This is a website. It works in the browser.
- **No newsletter popup.** If the content is good, they'll come back. If it's not, a newsletter won't save it.
- **No "How it works" page.** The show *is* how it works.
