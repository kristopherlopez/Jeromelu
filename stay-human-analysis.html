Section 1

Core Claim

The danger of AI is not using it too much but losing authorship of your own goals without noticing.

The book is not a guide to AI tools. It does not cover prompting, model selection, or workflow optimisation. It does not argue against using AI. It does not predict where AI is heading.

Mechanism: AI blends into your thinking (Blending), your identity attaches to what AI lets you do (Bonding), and your meaning frameworks flex around AI outputs (Bending). When these three shifts happen without external accountability, you drift from authorship into autopilot. The drift is invisible from inside because the outputs look good. Only other humans who watch you think can see the gap between producing and understanding. This holds when AI interaction is frequent, when no peer review is scheduled, and when the task involves judgment rather than execution.

Section 2

Key Concepts

Three interlocking frameworks form a system. The shifts say what is happening to you. The states say where you are. The roles say what position you have given AI.

The three shifts

Thinking

Blending

How AI responses mix into your thinking until you cannot separate yours from its. The clinical coder uses it productively. The developer who autopiloted does not. Same depth, opposite awareness.

Function: explains how understanding gets borrowed without the borrower noticing. Remove it: the competence illusion has no mechanism.
Identity

Bonding

How your sense of self gets tied to AI. The ADHD artist cried with relief. The novelist keeps AI away from her writing. The programmer chose the bond and chose what to withhold. Same phenomenon, different relationships.

Function: explains why people resist changing their AI setup even when it is not working. Remove it: the back-face roles (Outsourcer, Builder, Creator, Co-Author) lose their foundation.
Meaning

Bending

Your capacity to revise your frameworks when reality stops fitting them. The content creator who rebuilt from creator to curator. The grad student who holds both truths without resolving them.

Function: moderates everything. Without it, high Blending is drift and high Bonding is substitution. Remove it: the cube collapses from three dimensions to two. No way to distinguish productive from destructive integration.

The five states

1

Wake-Up

When AI becomes real. Wonder, dread, or both. Some people orbit this for months.

Signs: still surprised. Showing other people. Not yet settled.

2

Groove

AI becomes infrastructure. Efficient, unremarkable. The most dangerous state because it feels settled. You stop watching. Can drift into Merge without your noticing.

Check-in: when did you last ask whether your groove has shifted somewhere you did not intend?

3

Merge

The boundary dissolves. Can feel identical to the Groove when you are not paying attention. The clinical coder is in the Merge and knows it. The developer was in it and did not.

Diagnostic: can you explain your reasoning, not just your output, to someone who was not in the AI conversation?

4

Breaking

Something gives way. Can come from your own AI use, from AI's presence in the environment, or from market forces.

Signs: exhaustion, confusion, anger, grief about work. Avoiding certain conversations.

5

Rebuild

Constructing new frameworks. Key insight: AI is most useful where you have no expertise. The danger is every area where you do not know enough to notice AI got it wrong.

Signs: can explain their reasoning. Has thought deliberately about where AI fits.

The eight roles

Three axes create a cube. These are positions, not identities. You move between them.

Front face: AI stays outside your identity
Doer
Low Blending · Low Bonding · Low Bending

AI as simple tool

Bounded tasks. Transactional. Safe, but Edwards admits the skill of tidying her own thoughts is slipping, and it always seems like a tomorrow problem.

Function: baseline position. Shows what minimal AI integration looks like. Remove it: no reference point for measuring deeper integration.
Framer
High Blending · Low Bonding · Low Bending

You think, AI structures

You do the theory of mind. AI handles language and structure. The clinical coder is the archetype.

Function: shows productive high Blending is possible when identity stays separate. Remove it: no positive example of deep cognitive integration without identity entanglement.
Catalyst
Low Blending · Low Bonding · High Bending

AI sparks, you curate

AI generates possibilities. You select and verify. The fantasy writer who never tells AI which ideas she liked. Authorship stays pure. The ceiling may be lower.

Function: shows the cost of protecting purity. Remove it: the framework has no way to talk about the trade-off between protection and potential.
Partner
High Blending · Low Bonding · High Bending

Deep collaboration, identity intact

Works for people crossing disciplines. Key risk: the knowledge illusion.

Function: the front-face ceiling. Shows how far integration can go without identity entanglement. Remove it: no bridge between the safe front face and the risky back face.
Back face: AI becomes part of who you are
Outsourcer
Low Blending · High Bonding · Low Bending

The bond is emotional

"Things" AI handles has quietly expanded to include relating, processing, listening. Dependency builds one convenience at a time.

Function: shows how Bonding operates without cognitive depth. The emotional route to drift. Remove it: no way to explain non-cognitive dependency.
Builder
High Blending · High Bonding · Low Bending

Deep integration, stable domains

The funeral director who feels "somewhat replaceable." Integration happened task by task. No single moment of decision.

Function: shows what happens when integration is deep but frameworks are rigid. The magpie risk. Remove it: no account of how someone can be deeply integrated and stuck at the same time.
Creator
Low Blending · High Bonding · High Bending

AI produces, you keep voice

Dave dropped Midjourney. The tool was impressive. It did not let him feel like the creator. The feeling mattered more than the output.

Function: shows identity protection can hold even when Bonding is high, if Bending is high enough. Remove it: the argument that Bending moderates everything loses its strongest case.
Co-Author
High Blending · High Bonding · High Bending

Full integration, authorship intact

About 4% of participants. What united them: iterative back-and-forth, editorial control, language like "director" and "creative partner." Cannot be sustained alone.

Function: the aspiration point. Shows the goal is not less AI but more structure around AI. Remove it: the book has no positive vision of where deep integration leads.

Theory of Mind gap

AI builds its model from what you type. Humans who have watched you live can see what you hide from yourself. Dave knows Edwards rationalises overwork as dedication. He calls it out because he has watched her pay costs for it. AI does not know what she hides from herself because she does not type it.

Function: grounds the accountability argument in something concrete. Explains why other humans are structurally necessary, not just helpful. Remove it: "schedule the humans" has no justification beyond discipline.

Section 4

What to Trust

Solid
The competence illusion

Well documented in cognitive science independent of AI. AI amplifying it is a plausible extension.

Solid
Andy Clark's extended mind

Established philosophy of mind. Cognition flowing into tools is not new or contested.

Solid
Theory of Mind as a human capacity built from observation over time

Well-established developmental psychology. AI cannot replicate this from text alone.

Plausible
The three shifts framework

Conceptually coherent. Grounded in real observations. Not empirically validated. The three-axis structure is Edwards and Edwards, not prior research.

Plausible
Same technology, diverging futures

Real cases, consistent pattern. But self-selected stories. Causal claims cannot be drawn.

Plausible
The five states sequence

Observationally grounded. A map from cross-sectional observation, not a proven developmental path.

Plausible
Accountability collapse as the mechanism behind authorship loss

The Chronicle vs cube comparison is one personal case. Compelling, but one data point.

Speculative
The 4% Co-Author figure

Only hard number in the book. No methodology described. Treat as indicative, not precise.

Speculative
The economy shifting from attention to intimacy

Asserted. No evidence cited. A directional claim, not a measured finding.

Overall verdict: The observational patterns are real and consistent. The frameworks are plausible and useful. The causal claims are not supported by the evidence base. A well-observed map, not a scientific report.

Section 5

Apply It

  • Schedule the humans before you go deep

    Before starting a major AI work session, name who will review your thinking afterwards. Put it in your calendar. If you know you will have to explain your reasoning to a specific person, you work differently. Do not rely on discipline. Build the checkpoint in before you start. From Ch 1 and 6.

  • Write down what you want before you open AI

    One sentence: what am I trying to figure out? At the end, return to it. Did you get there, or did you get what AI gave you and call it good enough? Takes ten seconds. From Ch 1.

  • Walk someone through your reasoning without the transcript

    After AI-assisted work, explain what you did and why to someone who was not in the conversation. Do not open the chat. If you can reconstruct the logic, the understanding is yours. From Ch 3 and 7.

  • Map where AI helps, hurts, and misleads in your domain

    The lecturer's rule: one third useful, one third not helpful, one third actively wrong. Write your own version before you need it. From Ch 7.

  • Name the humans who know your mind

    Write down who has years of watching you think. If you struggle to name them, that is the most important information in the book. From Ch 7.

Conversations to have

With someone in the Wake-Up

What helps

"What are you finding? What's surprised you?"

Do not

Jump to warnings. Be the person who was curious.

With someone in the Groove

What helps

"Has anything surprised you lately about how AI is affecting your work?"

With someone whose self-concept is coming from AI

What helps

"That's interesting that AI said that about you. Does it match what other people have told you?"

With yourself, after any major AI session

Three questions

Did I get what I actually wanted, or what AI gave me? Can I explain my reasoning without the transcript? When did I last do something the slow way, just to know I still could?

Section 7

Stress Test

Compression

The people who thrive with AI stay the authors of their own questions. That requires other humans holding you accountable, not discipline.

Translation

Edwards' example: she spent hours generating frameworks with AI, felt productive, could not explain any of it to Dave.

Your version: the last time you used AI to prepare for a meeting, did you walk in with your own view or with AI's view that you had adopted? If your manager pushed back, could you defend your reasoning without the chat open? That is the same gap. Find one instance from this week.

Prediction

If this book is right, in two to three years you will see a growing split between professionals who can explain their reasoning and those who cannot. Job interviews, client calls, and performance reviews will increasingly reveal the gap between people who own their thinking and people who lease it. The first generation of workers who built their expertise entirely with AI will start hitting walls when asked to reason without it.

Opposition

The book assumes cognitive sovereignty is achievable with the right practices. A sharp critic would ask: what if the design of these systems makes authorship structurally impossible at scale? The accountability structures require other people with time and willingness. Most people do not have that.

The evidence base is self-selected stories from people willing to share. The patterns are real. Whether they are universal is an open question the book does not address. And the single most important finding, the 4% Co-Author figure, has no described methodology. The book's one hard number is also its weakest.
